# U.S. List Taxonomy for Zhejiang Sanctions Monitoring

## Purpose

Use this reference when deciding whether a U.S. list entry is a blocking sanction, export-control restriction, defense procurement restriction, investment restriction, or communications procurement restriction.

## OFAC SDN

**Agency:** U.S. Treasury, Office of Foreign Assets Control.

**What it does:** Blocks property and interests in property of listed persons/entities under U.S. jurisdiction and generally prohibits U.S. persons from transactions with them.

**Common row fields to preserve:** program tags, legal authority, aliases, addresses, IDs, “Linked To”, “Subject to Secondary Sanctions”, source action page, raw entry.

**For Zhejiang work:** SDN is the highest-priority list for emergency response because it can trigger banking, supply-chain, customer, and secondary-sanctions concerns.

## BIS Entity List

**Agency:** U.S. Commerce Department, Bureau of Industry and Security.

**What it does:** Imposes export-control licensing requirements for U.S.-origin items, software, and technology. License policy may be presumption of denial or case-by-case.

**What it does not usually do:** It does not by itself freeze assets or prohibit all U.S. persons from transactions.

**For Zhejiang work:** Focus on supply-chain and technology access impact, especially chips, software, equipment, and technical services.

## DoD 1260H / CMC

**Agency:** U.S. Department of Defense.

**What it does:** Identifies “Chinese Military Companies” operating directly or indirectly in the United States under Section 1260H. It is a national-security identification list and now interacts with U.S. defense procurement restrictions.

**Typical basis:** U.S. government assessment that the company is a military-civil fusion contributor, connected to the PLA, affiliated with PRC defense-industrial institutions, or otherwise tied to Chinese military modernization.

**Key effect:** It is not an OFAC asset-freeze list. The practical effects are defense procurement exclusion, third-party supply-chain screening, reputational risk, and compliance reviews by U.S. or multinational counterparties.

**For Zhejiang work:** Keep 1260H entries in a separate category from SDN. Do not count them as blocking sanctions unless the same entity is also on SDN or another blocking list.

## NS-CMIC

**Agency:** U.S. Treasury / OFAC.

**What it does:** Restricts U.S. persons from certain securities transactions involving identified Chinese military-industrial complex companies.

**For Zhejiang work:** Treat as investment and capital-market restriction, not the same as SDN blocking.

## FCC Covered List

**Agency:** U.S. Federal Communications Commission.

**What it does:** Identifies communications equipment/services that pose national security risk; restricts use of certain U.S. government funds for procurement or support.

**For Zhejiang work:** Relevant for telecoms, surveillance, and communications-equipment companies. It affects U.S. public-sector procurement and funding, not general asset freezing.

## State / ISN / DTC

**Agency:** U.S. State Department.

**What it does:** Includes nonproliferation, defense trade, arms export, or other list-specific restrictions.

**For Zhejiang work:** Preserve the exact source list and legal authority; do not infer SDN-style consequences without evidence.

## Comparison Rules

When comparing a generated dataset with a report:

1. Compare within the same list source first.
2. Compare all-list coverage only after explaining list-type differences.
3. Treat 2026-only questions as date-sensitive; older stock entries in reports should not be counted as missing from a 2026 additions table.
4. Track whether the dataset includes only additions, or also removals and changes.
5. Track whether the dataset includes vessels and aircraft.
6. Track whether foreign individuals linked to Chinese entities are in scope.
